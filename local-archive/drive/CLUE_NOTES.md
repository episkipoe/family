# Resolve the Signal — clue research notes

These notes distill recurring motifs from the locally cached travelogue and hacker-con corpus. They are design material, not a substitute for the source archive.

## Design rule

A satisfying clue should feel insufficient before the reveal and inevitable afterward. Prefer relationships, reversals, and recurring situations over dictionary definitions.

- One clue should leave several plausible options.
- Two clues should produce a theory, not certainty.
- Three clues should usually triangulate the answer.
- Context clues are shared across at least five answers so private history never becomes a one-person password.
- Structural clues remain option-balanced: five choices for an initial and three for a word length.

## Recurring hacker-con physics

- The queue becomes `LineCon`; waiting becomes participation.
- Hallways and elevators repeatedly become better social venues than scheduled rooms.
- A badge can be identity, hardware, currency, puzzle, invitation, or unreliable narrator.
- QR codes and USB drives are both obviously suspicious and nearly irresistible.
- Stickers, lanyards, ducks, and tiny pieces of swag acquire elaborate social lives.
- Goons enforce order while actively adding chaos, humor, and belonging.
- Soldering, lockpicking, firmware, and hostile media become welcoming hands-on activities.
- Hot dogs, pizza, towels, pool noodles, and beach balls become ceremonial under sleep deprivation.
- “Official” and “homemade” are not opposites; the homemade thing may carry more status.

## Implemented answer constellation

`badge`, `goon`, `village`, `sticker`, `lanyard`, `lockpick`, `soldering iron`, `QR code`, `duck`, `beach ball`, `towel`, `hot dog`, `hallway`, `elevator`, `USB drive`, `challenge`

Shared clue families include:

- “may identify you, recruit you, or send you somewhere worse”
- “more inviting here than it would be at an airport”
- “makes considerably more sense after prolonged sleep deprivation”
- “the real event has repeatedly happened around this by accident”
- “trusting it is optional; interacting with it apparently is not”
- “small enough to trade, important enough to start a story”
